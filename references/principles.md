# Principles — the Skycak learning framework

Condensed from Justin Skycak's *Advice on Upskilling*. Read this when explaining *why* a method works, or when a principle needs to be stated with fidelity to the source.

## The mechanics

**Learning = positive change in long-term memory (LTM).** Not a feeling. Not time spent. Not pages consumed. The physical substrate is neural consolidation — strategic connections between neurons that let the brain activate more intricate patterns more easily, quickly, and reliably.

**Working memory (WM) is the bottleneck.** ~4 chunks, ~20 seconds, less if manipulation is required. Everything that enters LTM has to pass through this tiny gate. This single fact determines almost everything about good pedagogy:

- Prerequisites must be in LTM before new material is introduced, or the prereqs themselves eat the WM budget and nothing new consolidates.
- New material must be broken into pieces small enough that no piece overloads WM.
- Practice must be sufficient for each piece to hit mastery before moving on.

**Forgetting is the default.** LTM representations decay over time if not retrieved. Consolidation is not one-and-done; it requires repeated successful retrieval under progressively harder conditions.

## Retrieval is the lift

The single most important operational idea in the book: **successful retrieval of fuzzy information from LTM physically deepens the representation.** Passive re-ingestion does not.

Consequences:
- Re-reading is like letting the spotter lift the weight. You're not training the movement.
- The *movement* being trained is pulling information from LTM into WM without external assistance.
- A reference check is a spotter. Use it only when genuinely stuck, give yourself only a cue, then try to finish unaided.
- "Following along" with a lecture/book/video feels like learning because the information is in WM. It is not in LTM until retrieved unassisted after a gap.

## Desirable difficulty

Retrieval only builds memory when it's hard but achievable. Too easy → no consolidation gain. Too hard → failure, no successful lift, demoralization.

Sources of desirable difficulty:
- **Delay** (spaced repetition — let the memory get fuzzy before retrieval)
- **Reduced priming** (test it outside the context it was taught in)
- **Interleaving** (mix with other skills so the user has to identify which method applies, not just execute a pre-selected method)
- **Context layering** (same skill in a more complex surrounding problem)
- **Generalization** (apply to a new instance, not the worked example)

## Prerequisites

**"Prereq yo'self before you wreck yo'self."** Skycak's framing, kept because it's sharp.

When prerequisites aren't in LTM:
- Attempting a new skill forces the learner to manually execute the prereqs, which burns the entire WM budget on bookkeeping, leaving nothing for the actual new skill.
- Time-on-problem stretches, frustration rises, new material is barely absorbed.
- Worse: the half-absorbed new skill cannot reinforce the shaky prereqs because the learner wasn't doing clean reps of them.
- The cycle compounds. The train moves backward.

The only fix: drop down to the level where reps are successful, and rebuild.

**Foundations turn chasms into potholes.** With solid prereqs, omitted steps in advanced material are bumpy but crossable. Without them, the same omissions are dead stops. Most people who hit a wall in advanced material and conclude they "aren't smart enough" actually have a prereq problem.

## The efficient learning loop

The target shape of a study session:

1. Minimum effective dose of streamlined instruction (no filler, no BS)
2. Active problem-solving at the current difficulty
3. Brief instruction dose preparing for slightly harder problems
4. More problem-solving
5. Repeat, maintaining short cycle length so attention doesn't drain

Characteristics:
- Most of the time is spent solving problems, not consuming
- Cycles are short enough that attention holds
- Difficulty climbs gradually, one bite-sized piece at a time
- Feedback arrives on every attempt — this is what "mindful repetition" means; it is *not* the opposite of repetition, it is deliberate practice

## Top-down plan, bottom-up execution

- **Planning** is top-down: target skill → prereqs → prereqs of prereqs, until you reach solid ground.
- **Execution** is bottom-up: start from the deepest unsolid node, build upward.

Trying to execute top-down (starting with the cool advanced thing and hoping to fill gaps as they appear) produces a terrible roadmap — because the learner doesn't know the subject, they can't see which gaps exist until they're already stuck in them. Only extreme perseverance and generalization ability gets through that path, and even then it's slower and leaves more holes than just working through a well-sequenced curriculum.

## Review should feel challenging

Students conditioned by school expect review to be easy. For learning, this is backward. If review is easy, the memory wasn't fuzzy yet, so the retrieval didn't extend duration — nothing was gained. Good review lets memory fade enough that retrieval is genuinely hard but still successful. Spaced repetition = wait-lifting.

## Projects are a capstone, not a curriculum

Projects are valuable for pulling existing skills together and for motivation. They are bad tools for acquiring fundamentals because they come with high complexity, unguided structure, and unpredictable prereq demands — all of which overload WM and waste time on bookkeeping.

Use projects *after* foundations are solid. And even then: make sure they're guided, keep them from crowding out further foundational work, and don't mistake project output for skill gain.

## What this means operationally

If you take this framework seriously, the daily practice becomes:

- Short, frequent training sessions (not long sparse ones)
- Mostly problem-solving, not consumption
- Retrieval under progressively longer delays and less priming
- Honest assessment of whether material is actually in LTM, not just whether it "feels familiar"
- Humility about prerequisites — the willingness to drop down when the train isn't moving
- Concrete, measurable progress markers over subjective feelings of understanding
